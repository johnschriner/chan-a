Okay, since you don't seem to know how it works, quick crash course. Skip past the setup concept art stage, that's pretty much identical between 2D and CGI works, right up till they're ready to make the first model. Okay?

2D, start drawing and start animating, that's ready to go.

CGI? First off, we ship off the final design to the modelers, the guys who are going to sculpt this thing out of the finished stages. Gotta get as many details down as you can, changing this later is VERY hard for reasons I'll get into later.

Okay, all made up and modeled? Good, got it. Now we ship it off to the Texture artists, who are going to make all the skin and clothing and whatever, anything not the model has to be textured and you have to get this texture right. Metal is by far the easiest to do, because organic textures have to take into account it's going to stretch slightly, like skin, which looks weird if the texture isn't made right. This is why robots are, comparatively, a breeze, especially when they're in anime and they don't have to work on 'realistic looking' metals, they can do flat colors with a reflective shader and boom, so much easier. Not so much if you want to do a face, and there's another reason for that.

cont
















Okay, your modeled, you're textured, and for arguments sake we're going to say all the shaders have been preprogrammed in, now we get to move on to the riggers! Any part of that thing you want to have the ability to move HAS to be properly rigged, and weighted. If it isn't, that model is going to deform and look terrible. There are ways to cut costs, such as giving people stick figure limbs like Illumination does with it's Despicable Me franchise, but facial animation is going to take a ton of practice to get perfect, otherwise you're going to look awful.

And then, once you're all rigged up, got your weights, sent it back for some texture touch-ups because this and that didn't deform right, and everything looks nice and natural, walla! You can now ship it off to an animator who can use his marrionette skills to move every single bone and weight in accordance with how the character is to be animated, and he can cycle through unrendered scenes and do touchups before being shipped off to the good old render farm, where it will take 40-90 hours to render a single high definition frame, depending on the budget of the film. Unless you're a live action movie, then it gets even higher.

And that is for ONE MODEL.

Now, here's the part where people get confused, I want you to imagine that process, done for literally every, single, solitary character. All of them. Anything that moves in the scene, period, from doors to levers to anything that moves, has to be rigged, if it breaks the inside of it has to be textured to look like a convincing break, if it crumbles it has to look like rubble, and any final models have to be made long before any of this can happen and be put into a scene.

See why people stick to 'robots' now?
