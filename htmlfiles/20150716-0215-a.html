Quick reminder: Shonen Jump's end-of-magazine survey only asks which series readers want to see more of, not about the actual quality of the material itself.

The ultimate goal of a Shonen Jump manga is not to actually be good, but to keep people reading until the next chapter comes out. In other words, to run as long as possible and keep interest along the way.

Which makes sense. After all, WSJ can't waste money running a series nobody cares about. Essentially, a series only dies when talk about it becomes nonexistant. This puts into perspective how many authors try to keep reader appeal:
Oda includes dozens of dangling plot lines, ensuring every reader has at least one conflict they want closure to. He also drags out arcs slightly (see Dressrosa), which helps.
Kishimoto used shocking swerves to keep up interest, whether good or bad. While we might think Naruto hyperventilating over Sasuke or cuckday was stupid, but it made you want to read the next chapter. And in the end, that's what mattered.
Kubo tries dragging out arcs much like Oda, as well as introducing new characters in the hopes one becomes a fan favorite and people want to see more of them. But his writing isn't interesting enough to let this work.
Double Arts was cancelled after only 22 chapters because it really didn't keep people hyped chapter-to-chapter. Sure, it had an ultimate conflict, but I wouldn't say it had anything that gave off the "I HAVE to know what's next" feeling.
Komi's next manga, Nisekoi, tries to replicate this feeling through bait-and-switching - introduce interesting characters, make readers empathize with them, and then blueball them for 150+ chapters. Sure, it's horrible, but as long as the otter eats the melon his work is done.
